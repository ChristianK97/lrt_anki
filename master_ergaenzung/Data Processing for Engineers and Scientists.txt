What is the<b> relative frequency</b>?	\(n\)&nbsp;samples with \(n_B\)&nbsp;discrete values \(v_i\) of classes \(C_i\), respectively, and the counts \(N_i (i=1,…,n_B)\)&nbsp;of samples in the respective categories such that \(\sum_{i=1}^{n_B}N_i=n\)&nbsp;holds.<br><br>The relative frequency of value&nbsp;\(v_k\) or class \(C_i&nbsp;\) is the relative count \((1\leq k\leq n_B)\)<br>\(P_k=\frac{N_k}{n}\in[0;1]\)<br><br>It represents the relative probability of finding a discrete value \(v_k\)&nbsp;&nbsp;or elements in class \(C_k\), respectively, when uniformly selecting one of the observed samples.		
What is the <b>cumulative frequency</b>?	\(n\)&nbsp;samples with \(n_B\)&nbsp;discrete values \(v_i\) of classes \(C_i\), respectively, and the counts \(N_i (i=1,…,n_B)\)&nbsp;of samples in the respective categories such that \(\sum_{i=1}^{n_B}N_i=n\)&nbsp;holds.<br><div><br></div><div>The cumulative frequency \(F_k\)&nbsp;is defined as \((1 \leq k \leq n_B)\):<br>\(F_k=\sum_{i=1}^{k}P_i \in [0;1]\)<br></div><div><br></div><div>It represents the probability of finding a value \(y \leq v_k\)&nbsp;or \(y \leq u_k\), respectively, under the assumption of uniformly selecting data \(y\) from all available samples.<br></div>		
What are <b>location parameters</b>?	Location parameters characterize the positioning of the observed data (relative to the axes).&nbsp;<div>They are sensitive to constant offsets or shifts of the data.</div>		
How are&nbsp;<b>arithmetic mean</b>&nbsp;and<b>&nbsp;weighted mean&nbsp;</b>defined?	"The <b style="""">arithmetic mean</b> is defined as<br>\(\bar{y}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}y_i\)<br><br>For <b style="""">binned/classified data</b> it is approximated by<br>\(\bar{y} \approx \sum_{k=1}^{n_B} P_k \frac{u_k+l_k}{2}=\sum_{k=1}^{n_B}&nbsp;P_k c_k\)<div><br><br>If the samples have weights \(w_i \geq 0\) then the <b style="""">weighted mean </b>is defined as<br>\(\bar{y}=\frac{1}{v}\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i y_i\), \(v=\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i\)</div>"

How are standard deviation and variance defined?	"The sample standard deviation \(s_n \geq 0\)&nbsp;and its square (the variance \(\sigma^2\)) are defined through<br><div>\(s_n = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{n−1}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i−\bar{y})^2 } \)</div><div><br><div></div><div>The standard deviation \(s_n\) expresses the scatter of the data with respect to the <a href=""https://ilias3.uni-stuttgart.de/ilias.php?ref_id=2173641&amp;obj_id=459444&amp;obj_type=PageObject&amp;cmd=layout&amp;cmdClass=illmpresentationgui&amp;cmdNode=fz&amp;baseClass=ilLMPresentationGUI"">arithmetic mean</a>. What is the <b>skewness</b>&nbsp;of a distribution?	The third central moment (normalized) defines the <b>skewness</b><br><div>\(\text{skew}(\underline{y})=\frac{M_c^3(\underline{y})}{s^3}\)</div><div><br></div><div><div></div><div>- the skewness is a dimensionless number</div><div>- it is invariant under shifting and scaling, i.e. it describes the shape of the distribution</div><div>- for \(\text{skew} &lt;0\) the distribution is left-skewed and for \(\text{skew}&gt;0\) it is right-skewed</div><div>- left-skewed data has the mean left of the median which is found left of the mode</div><div>- right-skewed data has the mean right of the median which is found right of the mode</div></div>		
What is the&nbsp;<b>kurtosis</b>&nbsp;of a distribution?	The fourth normalized moment defines the <b>(excess) kurtosis</b> via<br><div>\(\text{kurt}(\underline{y})=\frac{M_c^4(\underline{y})}{s^4}−3\)</div><div><br><div></div><div>- the substracted value of 3 is the fourth central moment of the normal distribution, i.e. the kurtosis compares the shape of the distribution w.r.t. the normal distribution</div><div>- positive kurtosis --&gt; data is more spread than for the normal distribution</div><div>- negative kurtosis --&gt; data is more concentrated around the mean value than for normally distributed samples</div></div>		
What are <b>shape parameters</b>?	<b>Shape parameters </b>describe the shape of a distribution, similar to, e.g.,  curvature for functions. These parameters are independent of shifts.<div>In  general, dimensionless shape parameters should also be  de-dimensionalized, making them insensitive to rescaling of the data.</div>		
How is the <b>k-th central moment</b> defined? How ist it normalized?	The<b> k-th central moment</b> is defined for \(k\in{2,3,…}\)&nbsp;as<br>\(M_c^k(\underline{y})=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i−\bar{y})^k\)<div><br>Dividing \(M_c^k\) by \(s_k\), the <b>k-th normalized moment</b> is obtained. It is <b>dimensionless</b>.</div>		
"Explain the ""dilemma of continuous probability spaces!"	Let \(\Omega=[0,1]\)&nbsp;and assert that all numbers on the unit interval should be <i>equally probable.</i>  Since there exist infinitely many (i.e. not even countable!) real  numbers, each individual number must have the probability of \(0\).<br><br>Having a zero probability for each singleton \(P({x})=0\)&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;\(∀x∈[0,1]\)&nbsp;induces that the sum of the probability over all events will be zero, as well.
